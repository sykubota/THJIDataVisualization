A Brief History

While internment via camps have been used many times throughout history and have been called many different names, two of the ones most well known to the Western world are the Holocaust and the Japanese Internment, which both happened during the second World War between 1933 and 1946.

In Europe, while Anti-Semitic attitudes existed long before World War 2, it wasn’t a belief held by the general population. With the rise of the Nazi party in Germany, many became apathetic towards the Jewish population, which the Nazi party took advantage of to reinforce their Anti-Semetic propoganda.

In the US, racial tensions were already on the rise, particularly towards groups of Asian descent. While the Chinese Exclusion Act wasn’t aimed toward all Asians, the prejudice behind the support of that law wasn’t limited to those of Chinese descent. This was reinforced by Japan rising to the status of world power.

In addition, the rise of Eugenics in the 20s and 30s definitely reinforced prejudices throughout the Western world as a whole.

Introduction

The United States and Germany both made numerous decisions to remove power from people they viewed as threats. As you explore the timeline, notice how the events for both the Holocaust and Japanese Internment started, peaked, and ended around the same time. However, the Japanese Internment events started off much slower than Holocaust events.
